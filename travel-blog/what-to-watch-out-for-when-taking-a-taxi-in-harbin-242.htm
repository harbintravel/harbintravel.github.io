What to Watch Out for When Taking a Taxi in Harbin

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

Harbin, the capital of Heilongjiang province in northeastern China, is known for its icy winters, Russian-influenced architecture, and the world-famous Harbin Ice Festival. While public transportation options such as buses and the subway are available, many visitors and locals prefer taking taxis for convenience, especially during the frigid winter months. However, taking a taxi in Harbin requires some awareness of local customs, common issues, and potential pitfalls.

Understanding Harbin’s Taxi System

Harbin has a well-established taxi network, and cabs are relatively affordable compared to major cities like Beijing and Shanghai. The taxis in Harbin are usually brightly colored, with the most common models being Hyundai Elantra, Volkswagen Jetta, and Toyota Corolla. The standard taxi fare starts at around ¥9 for the first 3 kilometers, with an additional per-kilometer charge after that.

There are also ride-hailing services such as Didi available in Harbin, which provide an alternative to traditional taxis. However, during peak hours or bad weather, it can be challenging to book a car through an app.

Avoiding Unlicensed Taxis

One of the biggest concerns when taking a taxi in Harbin is avoiding unlicensed or illegal taxis, often referred to as “hei che” (黑车). These unregistered vehicles operate without official oversight and may charge exorbitant fares. They are commonly found at tourist hotspots, transportation hubs, and major shopping districts.

To ensure you are getting into a licensed taxi, look for the following:

  • A proper taxi license plate with a clearly marked identification number
  • A working taximeter
  • A driver’s identification card displayed on the dashboard
  • The official taxi company logo on the vehicle

If a driver approaches you at an airport or train station offering a ride without a meter, it is best to decline and find a registered taxi.

Dealing with Overcharging and Route Manipulation

While most Harbin taxi drivers are honest, some may try to overcharge passengers, especially if they detect you are a tourist. Here are some common tactics and how to avoid them:

Refusing to Use the Meter

Some drivers may claim the meter is broken and suggest a fixed price instead. This price is often much higher than what the metered fare would be. Always insist on using the meter. If the driver refuses, it is better to find another taxi.

Taking Longer Routes

Some taxi drivers deliberately take longer routes to increase the fare, especially if they assume you are unfamiliar with the city. To prevent this:

  • Use navigation apps like Baidu Maps or Amap to track your route.
  • If you suspect the driver is taking an unnecessarily long route, politely ask why.
  • Mention that you are tracking the ride—this often discourages drivers from attempting any tricks.

Fake Extra Charges

A few dishonest drivers might try to charge extra fees, such as a “night surcharge” beyond the official rate or additional baggage fees. It is best to be familiar with the city’s standard taxi fare structure to avoid such scams.

Handling Taxi Shortages in Winter

Harbin’s winter is harsh, with temperatures often dropping below -20°C (-4°F). During peak hours and extreme cold spells, taxis become highly sought after, making it difficult to find one.

Best Ways to Secure a Taxi in Winter

  • Use a Taxi-Hailing App: Didi and similar apps increase your chances of getting a taxi, especially if you choose priority options.
  • Head to Major Roads or Hotels: Taxis are more likely to be available near hotels, shopping malls, and main roads rather than smaller streets.
  • Be Prepared to Share a Taxi: In some cases, if a taxi is heading in your direction, the driver may pick up multiple passengers along the way.

Taxi Behavior in Cold Weather

Some drivers may refuse short-distance trips in winter, preferring longer fares due to the difficulty of starting and stopping in icy conditions. Others may try to negotiate a fixed fare instead of using the meter. If you experience this, remain firm in requesting a metered ride or try another cab.

Communication Challenges and Language Barriers

Many taxi drivers in Harbin do not speak English, so if you are not fluent in Mandarin, communication may be challenging. Here are some ways to overcome this barrier:

  • Have Your Destination Written in Chinese Characters: Show it to the driver on a phone or piece of paper.
  • Use a Translation App: Apps like Google Translate or Baidu Translate can help facilitate communication.
  • Learn Basic Phrases: Knowing a few key phrases in Mandarin, such as “qǐng yòng biǎo” (请用表, "Please use the meter") or “wǒ dào le” (我到了, "I have arrived"), can be useful.

Common Taxi Etiquette in Harbin

Understanding local taxi etiquette can make your ride smoother and more pleasant:

  • Sit in the Back: Most passengers sit in the back seat, though sitting in the front is acceptable if the back is full.
  • Pay in Cash or Mobile Payment: While taxis accept cash, many also support WeChat Pay and Alipay. Ensure you have some small bills if paying in cash, as drivers may not always have change for large denominations.
  • No Tipping Culture: Unlike in some Western countries, tipping is not expected in China. However, rounding up the fare slightly is sometimes appreciated.
  • Fast Exits Are Expected: Drivers often prefer quick drop-offs, especially in busy areas. Have your belongings ready before arriving at your destination.

Special Considerations for Airport and Train Station Pickups

If you are arriving at Harbin Taiping International Airport or Harbin Railway Station, keep the following in mind:

Avoiding Airport Taxi Scams

  • Use the Official Taxi Queue: Ignore drivers who approach you inside the terminal offering rides.
  • Watch for Fake Taxis: Some unlicensed drivers use cars that resemble real taxis but lack proper documentation.
  • Verify the Fare: From the airport to downtown Harbin, a metered taxi ride should cost approximately ¥120–¥150, depending on traffic and exact location. If a driver quotes a significantly higher price, find another taxi.

Taking a Taxi from the Train Station

  • Expect Crowds: Harbin’s main train stations can be chaotic, especially during peak travel seasons.
  • Watch for Unauthorized Drivers: The same advice for avoiding airport scams applies here.
  • Be Prepared for Cold Weather: If waiting outside, dress warmly to withstand the frigid temperatures.

Alternative Transportation Options

If taxis are difficult to find or if you prefer other options, consider these alternatives:

  • Didi Ride-Hailing: Works similarly to Uber and allows for cashless payment.
  • Subway: Harbin’s subway system is expanding and is a reliable way to get around.
  • Buses: While cheap, buses can be crowded and confusing for non-locals.
  • Hotel Car Services: Many hotels offer private car services that, while more expensive, ensure a hassle-free ride.

By being aware of these key factors, you can navigate Harbin’s taxi system with greater confidence and avoid common pitfalls. Whether you are visiting for the Ice Festival or simply exploring the city’s rich cultural heritage, a smooth taxi experience will enhance your trip.
